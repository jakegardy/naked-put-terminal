The Monthly Put-Selling Strategy
A sequential guide to Chad's approach — from first principles to executing your first trade
01 — What You're Actually Doing
Selling insurance on stocks you'd be happy to own

When you sell a put option, you're taking on an obligation: if the stock falls below a certain price (the strike), you agree to buy 100 shares at that price. In exchange, someone pays you a premium upfront — cash in your account immediately, regardless of what happens next.

The key mental model is this: you're not speculating on stock direction. You're collecting rent for agreeing to buy a quality stock at a discount. The stocks in this universe are Dividend Kings and Aristocrats — companies that have raised their dividends for 25-50+ consecutive years. If you get assigned, you own something worth owning.

Chad's target is 2% per month compounded. At $50,000 that's $1,000/month. Compounded over 20 years at 2%/month it reaches extraordinary numbers — the math is what makes the discipline worth it.

The core mindset
Assignment is not a loss. It's acquiring a quality dividend stock at a price you already agreed was acceptable. The premium you collected reduces your cost basis further.
02 — The Monthly Cycle
One decision day per month. Everything else is waiting.

Monthly options expire on the 3rd Friday of every month. Chad executes his trades on the Monday after the previous month's 3rd Friday — giving him approximately 30 days of time value to collect.

1
3rd Friday — Expiry day
Your puts from last month expire. Either worthless (keep premium, done) or you're assigned shares (now own 100 shares per contract at your strike price). You find out Saturday morning via your broker.
2
Monday morning — Trade day
Open this tool. Fetch live prices and options chains. Review scored candidates. Select positions, confirm rungs, verify premiums. Execute trades before noon. This is your one active day of the month.
3
The next 30 days — Hold
Do nothing. The premium is already in your account. Time decay (theta) works in your favor every single day — the put loses value as expiry approaches, which is exactly what you want.
4
98% rule — Optional early close
If your put has lost 98% of its value before expiry (e.g. you sold for $500 and it's now worth $10), buy it back and free up the capital for the next cycle. Don't give back $10 to save two more weeks of assignment risk.
03 — Choosing Your Strike
The tradeoff between premium and probability

The strike price determines two things simultaneously: how much premium you collect, and the probability the put expires worthless. These move in opposite directions. The closer to the current price (ATM), the more premium — but the higher the chance of assignment.

Probability of Profit (PoP) is derived from the option's delta. A delta of 0.28 means approximately 28% chance of assignment — or 72% PoP. This is the metric that drives rung selection in this tool.

ATM
At the money
~50% PoP
Maximum premium. Coin flip odds of assignment. Chad uses this rarely — only in very low IV environments where you need to go close to generate meaningful income.
OTM1
1 strike below ATM
~60-65% PoP
Good premium, still meaningful assignment risk. Used in moderate IV months when you want more income than OTM2 provides.
OTM2
2 strikes below ATM
~72-78% PoP
Chad's default sweet spot. Strong premium with meaningful safety margin. The 72% PoP threshold is where risk and reward balance for this strategy.
OTM3
3 strikes below ATM
~80-85% PoP
Conservative months. Lower premium but high probability. Use when the market feels unstable or you're less confident in a specific name.
OTM4
4 strikes below ATM
~86%+ PoP
Maximum safety. Premium is thin but assignment risk is very low. Useful as a reference point — if OTM4 still generates meaningful income, it's a high IV month.
04 — The Three Outcomes
Every position ends one of three ways
Best case
Expires worthless
Stock stays above your strike through the 3rd Friday. Put expires with zero value. You keep 100% of the premium. Capital is freed and you sell new puts Monday. This happens roughly 72-83% of the time at Chad's preferred rungs.
Middle case
Assigned — you own the stock
Stock closes below your strike on expiry Friday. Saturday morning your broker notifies you — you now own 100 shares per contract at the strike price. Your cost basis is the strike minus premium collected. Immediately begin selling covered calls Monday to generate income while you hold. Collect dividends while waiting to be called away.
Worst case
Assigned at a significant loss
Stock drops sharply — 20%, 30%, more. You're assigned shares at a price well above current market value. This is why stock selection matters enormously. Dividend Kings and Aristocrats with strong balance sheets recover. You continue selling covered calls and collecting dividends until the position recovers or gets called away at breakeven. The premium and dividends reduce your cost basis over time.
Why quality matters in assignment
A Dividend King that drops 25% still pays its dividend and has 50+ years of proving it recovers. A speculative stock at -25% might never come back. This strategy only works long-term if you're comfortable owning every stock you sell puts on.
05 — If Assigned: The Covered Call Cycle
Turning an assigned position into an income machine

Once assigned, you own 100 shares per contract. Now you sell covered calls — the mirror image of what you've been doing. You agree to sell your shares at a higher price (above current market) and collect premium for that agreement.

The goal is to get called away at or above your cost basis. Each month you sell a covered call slightly above your cost basis. If it gets exercised (called away), you sell the shares at that price and your position closes profitably. If it doesn't, you keep the premium and try again next month.

Meanwhile the stock is paying dividends — quarterly income on top of your monthly covered call premium. An assigned position on a 4% yielder effectively becomes a dividend income position with bonus monthly premiums while you wait.

The covered call strike rule
Sell the covered call at or slightly above your cost basis (strike minus total premium collected). This ensures you profit on the stock appreciation if called away, plus you collect the covered call premium.
06 — Executing the Trade
Order mechanics — how to actually place the trade

When you're ready to sell a put, you're entering a Sell to Open order on the put contract at your chosen strike and expiry. Here's how to think through each field on the order ticket:

→
Order type: Limit order
Always use a limit order, never market. A market order on an options contract can result in a terrible fill. Set your limit price at the midpoint of the bid/ask spread. Example: bid $4.20, ask $4.80 — set limit at $4.50. These fills almost always execute on liquid dividend stocks.
→
Duration: Day order — not GTC
Chad's preference is Day orders, not Good Till Cancelled (GTC). If your limit doesn't fill by end of day, the order expires. This forces you to come back the next morning and re-evaluate — has the stock moved? Has IV changed? Is the midpoint still reasonable? A GTC order might fill days later at a price that no longer makes sense given what the market is doing.
→
Action: Sell to Open
You are opening a new short put position. "Sell to Open" creates the obligation. When you eventually close or it expires, that's "Buy to Close" (if you close early) or it simply expires and the obligation disappears.
→
Contracts: Match your tool selection
Enter the number of contracts from your Trade Planner. Remember each contract covers 100 shares. Your broker will show the cash-secured requirement — the capital that gets held as collateral. Confirm this matches your exposure calculation.
→
What to do if you don't get filled
Order expires end of day. Next morning — check where the stock opened, check the new bid/ask, recalculate midpoint, and decide if you still want the position. Sometimes the stock moved and the new midpoint is better. Sometimes IV dropped and it's no longer worth selling. The day order gives you that daily decision point.
07 — Position Sizing and Risk
The rules that keep you in the game

The single most important rule: you must be able to cover 100% of assignment on every put you sell simultaneously. If you sell 5 positions and all 5 get assigned on the same bad Friday, you need to be able to buy every single one. This is not a theoretical exercise — October 2008 happened. March 2020 happened.

!
Never exceed 100% coverage
The tool shows your coverage percentage in the Trade Builder. Keep it at or below 100%. Going over means you couldn't cover a full assignment event — that's margin territory and a different (more dangerous) strategy.
!
Diversify across sectors
Don't sell puts on 5 energy stocks. If oil drops, all 5 get assigned simultaneously. Spread across sectors — one energy, one consumer staple, one industrial, one financial. Correlation risk is real.
!
Beta sweet spot: 0.7 to 1.5
Below 0.7 and the stock is too stable — premiums are too thin to hit 2% monthly. Above 1.5 and the stock swings too hard — assignment risk becomes meaningful and recovery from a bad assignment takes too long. Chad's sweet spot generates enough premium while keeping assignment manageable.
!
Only sell puts on stocks you want to own
This is the foundational rule. If you wouldn't be comfortable holding the stock for 12-24 months through a downturn, don't sell a put on it. Assignment turns your put into a stock position. Chad's approved list reflects stocks he's genuinely willing to own.
